Homeostasis & The Kidneys
Kidney structure
Fig. 1 — Cross-section of the kidney showing cortex, medulla, and pelvis

The kidneys are not merely waste-removal organs; they function as regulatory systems. They maintain homeostasis by filtering blood to remove nitrogenous wastes such as urea, while also controlling the levels of water and electrolytes.

Conceptual focus: The key to understanding kidney function is recognizing that it involves selective control. The kidneys continuously adjust what is excreted and what is retained, depending on the body's internal conditions.

The Nephron
Nephron diagram
Fig. 2 — Structure of a nephron: glomerulus, Bowman's capsule, and renal tubule

The functional unit of the kidney is the nephron. Each nephron is specialized for precise processing of blood through a sequence of controlled steps.

Its main components include:

  • Glomerulus – a network of capillaries where high-pressure filtration occurs
  • Bowman's capsule – cup-like structure that receives the filtrate
  • Renal tubule – modifies the filtrate through reabsorption and secretion

Thinking model: View the nephron as a sequential processing system: initial filtration is broad and non-selective (for small molecules), followed by highly selective recovery and adjustment.

Urine Formation

Urine formation occurs through three coordinated processes:

  1. Ultrafiltration:
    Blood enters the glomerulus under high hydrostatic pressure, forcing water and small solutes (glucose, amino acids, salts, urea) into Bowman's capsule. Large molecules such as proteins and blood cells remain in circulation.
    Core idea: Non-selective filtration of small molecules.
  2. Selective Reabsorption:
    As the filtrate passes through the tubule, essential substances are reabsorbed back into the blood. This includes glucose, amino acids, and other important nutrients. The amount of water and ions is also reabsorbed.
    Core idea: Selective recovery based on physiological needs.
  3. Tubular Secretion:
    Additional substances (e.g., hydrogen ions, potassium ions, certain drugs) are actively transported from the blood into the tubule.
    Core idea: Final regulation of composition.
"

Filter → Reabsorb → Regulate — the three-step formula for understanding how urine is made.

Osmoregulation
ADH mechanism
Fig. 3 — ADH increases water permeability of the collecting duct

Beyond waste removal, the kidneys maintain osmotic balance—the proper concentration of water and dissolved substances in body fluids. This process is known as osmoregulation.

The permeability of the distal tubule and collecting duct to water is regulated by Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH):

  • High ADH levels → increased water reabsorption → concentrated urine
  • Low ADH levels → reduced water reabsorption → dilute urine

Thinking model: The kidneys function as a feedback-controlled system, adjusting output in response to internal signals rather than operating at a fixed rate.
